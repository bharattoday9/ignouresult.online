Discuss the nature of sustainable development

12 Dec, 2022 | Written by Naman Kumar

Sustainable development entails the use of resources in a way that meets the needs of current generations without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.

At its core, sustainable development seeks to provide a framework for making decisions about how to best use natural resources for the benefit of all people, now and in the future. This includes considering the impact of development on air and water quality, land use, wildlife and other natural resources, as well as the impact of development on public health, human rights and equity.

Nature of Sustainable Development and Its Key Elements

Key Elements of Sustainable Development are as follows:

  • Economic Development: Sustainable development focuses on making sure that economic growth is equitable, fair, and productive. This includes providing access to decent jobs, increasing access to resources for the world’s most vulnerable populations, and promoting entrepreneurship and innovation.
  • Social Development: Sustainable development seeks to ensure that all people have access to basic human rights and social services, such as education, health care and housing. It also encourages social inclusion and the protection of vulnerable communities.
  • Environmental Protection: Sustainable development recognizes the importance of preserving the environment and preserving natural resources. This includes promoting the use of renewable energy sources, reducing pollution, and protecting biodiversity.
  • Good Governance: Sustainable development requires strong and effective governance, with the participation of all stakeholders. This includes the implementation of laws and policies that promote resource management, economic development and environmental protection.

Details from IGNOU Self Learning Material (BPAG-174 Chapter 1)

We must consider the complete range of physical and human capital, as well as natural resources, that will determine our children's and grandchildren's wellbeing while keeping in mind what we leave behind or pass on to them. Adopting the sustainable development paradigm would necessitate a significant shift in perspective. Instead of only considering the short-term costs of profits associated with depleting resources that generate income, the data utilised for decision-making must take into account the true costs of resource depletion and pollution as they effect future generations. Future needs must be considered in the data on an equal footing with current needs, not "discounted" in a way that favours actions with immediate results.

By limiting human activities that make it unprofitable to stop or reverse environmental degradation, Ciricacy-Wantrup (1952) emphasised the use of safe minimum requirements for conservation. This would prevent over-exploitation of sensitive environmental zones. Consequently, the concept of sustainable development necessitates a detailed examination of sustainable threshold levels for both economic and environmental systems. For instance, deforestation may be vital for the growth of agriculture in a region's economy like Brazil, but it may be fatal and damaging to the ecological stability of the entire planet. An increase in Gross National Product (GNP) may conceal or mask serious development issues when a nation has rapid population expansion or extreme urbanisation.

When a country or region's natural resources are in high demand worldwide to fulfil expanding global needs, the same problem occurs. In conclusion, sustainability will remain an imaginary concept until we are ready to define it in terms that account for both internal threat from demographic pressure in the South and external threat from food policy in the North. Similar to how it is required to manage and control population growth rates, maintain balanced livestock development, and regulate land alienation to deal with India's developing concerns with land pressures. On the other hand, it is difficult to determine success rate when the World Commission states that "sustainable development necessitates that detrimental impacts on quality of air, water, & other natural elements are minimised, so as to protect ecosystem's overall integrity."

The World Commission has acknowledged these concerns and highlighted that in order to quantify sustainable development achievement, the context and the requirement to address social challenges must be taken into consideration. In order to be sustainable, environmental managers must strive for:

  1. maintaining ecological processes that are crucial for the health of the biosphere, such as the ecosystem;
  2. maintaining biological diversity by promoting and insuring the preservation of all species of flora and wildlife in their natural settings;
  3. observing the maximisation of sustainable production when using living ecosystems and natural resources;
  4. preventing or lessening serious environmental harm or pollution;
  5. establishing acceptable criteria for environmental protection;

The Brundtland Report from 1989 was thought to lack a precise definition of sustainability during the Rio Summit in 1992. A new definition has recently been developed by the World Bank's Environment Department. It has two sections: The following guidelines should be followed: I Output Guide: Waste emissions should not exceed the capacity of the local environment to absorb them without degradation; and (ii) Input Guide: Harvest rates for renewable resources should not exceed the rate at which they can be replenished naturally; depletion rates for non-renewable resources should not exceed the rate at which renewable substitutes are developed.

Conclusion

As you know already, Sustainable development is a term that refers to the development of economic, social and environmental systems in a way that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. It is the balancing act of managing resources in a way that allows for economic growth while also protecting the environment and ensuring social justice.

Sustainable development is a concept that seeks to ensure that economic, social and environmental systems are managed in a way that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. It is a complex concept that requires consideration of a wide range of factors, including economic development, social development, environmental protection, and good governance. It is an important concept for all nations to consider in order to ensure a future that is both economically prosperous and environmentally sustainable.
