Discuss the Green Accounts approach to sustainable development

13 Dec, 2022 | Written by Naman Kumar

I have taken this question from the IGNOU Self Learning Material of BPAG-174 (Sustainable Development) course. I have attempted to answer it properly.

The Green Accounts approach to sustainable development is an economic framework that aims to integrate environmental considerations into the decision-making process of individuals, businesses, and governments and involves considering the environmental costs and benefits of economic activities in order to make more informed decisions about how resources should be allocated.

Key Features of the Green Accounts Approach

One of the key features of the Green Accounts approach is the use of environmental accounting to measure the environmental impact of economic activities. This involves estimating the value of natural resources and the costs of environmental degradation, such as pollution and habitat destruction. These values can then be incorporated into the decision-making process, allowing for a more comprehensive evaluation of the costs and benefits of different economic activities.

Another important aspect of the Green Accounts approach is the emphasis on the concept of sustainable development. This involves meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. The Green Accounts approach promotes the idea of intergenerational equity, which means considering the interests of future generations when making decisions about the use of natural resources.

Tools and Techniques used in the Green Accounts Approach

Some of the specific tools and techniques used in the Green Accounts approach include:

  • Environmental impact assessments, which evaluate the potential environmental consequences of a proposed economic activity
  • Environmental accounting, which estimates the value of natural resources and the costs of environmental degradation
  • Eco-taxes, which are taxes on activities that have a negative environmental impact, such as pollution or deforestation
  • Subsidies for environmentally-friendly activities, such as renewable energy or conservation efforts
  • Market-based instruments, such as cap-and-trade systems, which help to internalize the costs of environmental degradation into the decision-making process of businesses and consumers.

Building a Green Economy With the Green Accounts Approach

Building a "Green Economy," which entails promoting social justice, human well-being, and the reduction of environmental dangers, is a goal shared by all countries. The importance of sustainability and the green economy has grown in emerging nations. The national accounts that account for environmental externalities are known as "green accounts." They aid in our comprehension of the environmental effects of our social, political, and economic actions. The idea of "green accounting" takes into account the environmental harm that human activity causes, which is not taken into account by the conventional accounting system. The SD difficulties are not addressed by the traditional national accounting system.

The Green Accounts approach to sustainable development (SD) can be integrated into decision-making by using the Transfer Model approach proposed by Paul P. Craig and Harold Glasser. This model emphasizes the importance of considering stocks and flows, as well as bio-geophysical aspects, when evaluating the environmental impact of economic activities. The concept of sustainable utilisation, which involves spending the interest while preserving the capital, is also central to this approach. In order for this model to be effective, certain baseline conditions must be met in bio-geophysical terms, such as ensuring access to clean water, healthy food, and habitat for species. Despite numerous studies and models on the topic, this approach offers a unique perspective on integrating environmental considerations into economic decision-making.

The Priciples of Sustainable Development (SD) Under the Green Accounts Approach

Two guiding principles were provided by economist Herman Daly for SD:

  1. the harvest rate must be equal to or lower than regenerative rates (sustained yield)
  2. the waste emission rates must be equal to or lower than the natural assimilative capacities of the ecosystems into which they are emitted.

These concepts' bio-geophysical components set them apart from conventional economic indicators. Only renewable resources cannot be a part of green accounting. The Transfer Model approach recommends incorporating uncertainty and various points of view (Craig, 1994).

Conclusion

Overall, the Green Accounts approach seeks to incorporate environmental considerations into economic decision-making in order to promote sustainable development and protect the interests of future generations. By considering the environmental costs and benefits of economic activities, this approach can help to ensure that natural resources are used in a way that is both efficient and sustainable.
