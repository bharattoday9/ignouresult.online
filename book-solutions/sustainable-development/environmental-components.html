Discuss the environmental components of sustainable development

12 Dec, 2022 | Written by Naman Kumar

The environmental components of sustainable development include efforts to protect and restore ecosystems, reduce pollution, and promote the sustainable use of natural resources.

Sustainable development is a concept that has become increasingly important in recent years as global populations and economic activity have grown. The concept emphasizes the need to balance economic growth, social equity, and the protection of the environment in order to ensure long-term prosperity. In this article, we will discuss the environmental components of sustainable development.

  • Natural Resources: Sustainable development requires the use of natural resources in ways that do not degrade the environment or deplete resources. This includes the use of renewable energy sources such as solar and wind, as well as the conservation of water and other resources in order to prevent their depletion.
  • Biodiversity: Sustainable development also requires preserving biodiversity in order to maintain a healthy and productive environment. This includes the protection of wildlife habitats and ecosystems, as well as the conservation of genetic resources.
  • Pollution: Sustainable development also requires reducing pollution in order to protect human health and the environment. This includes reducing air pollution from the burning of fossil fuels, as well as reducing water and soil pollution from industrial activities.
  • Climate Change: Climate change is another environmental component of sustainable development. This includes reducing emissions of greenhouse gases, such as carbon dioxide, in order to reduce the impacts of climate change.
  • Waste Management: Sustainable development also requires proper waste management in order to protect the environment. This includes reducing, recycling, and reusing materials in order to reduce the amount of waste that is produced and sent to landfills.
  • Renewable Energy: Sustainable development also requires the use of renewable energy sources in order to reduce dependence on fossil fuels. This includes the use of solar, wind, and geothermal energy in order to reduce emissions of greenhouse gases.
  • Sustainable Agriculture: Sustainable development also requires the use of sustainable agricultural practices in order to protect soil and water resources. This includes the use of integrated pest management, reduced tillage, and conservation tillage in order to reduce soil erosion and conserve water.
  • Sustainable Forestry: Sustainable development also requires the use of sustainable forestry practices in order to protect forests and their habitats. This includes the use of selective harvesting techniques and the protection of old-growth forests.
  • Sustainable Transportation: Sustainable development also requires the use of sustainable transportation methods in order to reduce air pollution and protect the environment. This includes the use of public transportation, walking and biking, and the use of hybrid and electric vehicles.

In conclusion, sustainable development requires the protection and conservation of the environment in order to ensure long-term prosperity. This includes the use of renewable energy sources, the protection of biodiversity, the reduction of pollution, the proper management of waste, the use of sustainable agricultural and forestry practices, and the use of sustainable transportation methods. By implementing these components of sustainable development, we can ensure a healthy and prosperous future.

Additional Information

The environment, which is being harmed by the negative effects of economic expansion and social inequality, is the third element of sustainable development. Due to their intimate ties, the three elements overlap one another. Cheap fossil fuels (coal, petroleum, and natural gas) have undoubtedly fueled rapid economic expansion, but they have also harmed the environment by emitting enormous amounts of greenhouse gases.

This is currently one of the main contributors to climate change. The Johannesburg Plan of Implementation for Sustainable Development initially placed a strong emphasis on:

  • sustainable energy and biomass use;
  • altering the unsustainable consumption and production practises, particularly in industrialised nations;
  • pursuing eco-efficiency and cleaner production methods;
  • inclusion of authorities and private firms;
  • Transportation, garbage, and chemicals are key topics;
  • preserving and managing the natural resources that are the foundation for social and economic development;
  • Infrastructure for health is being improved to combat the negative effects of environment-related harm;
  • economic and social integration in a globalising world;
  • assistance with technology, capacity building, coastal biodiversity, etc. for small island (developing) states.
  • regional initiatives in Latin America and Africa; and
  • methods of implementation and modernization of the institutional framework.

How are growth and environmental sustainability related?

Our attention was drawn to the relationship between sustainable development and poverty reduction in the Brundtland Report of the World Commission on Environment and Development (1987), with the former being a requirement for environmental protection and an input towards sustainable development. Sustainable development and the environment are intertwined, and this link defines how to minimise the damaging consequences of human activity on the ecosystem. The term "sustainable ecological perspective" refers to eco-development that will enhance quality of life, safeguard biodiversity, and preserve the environment. Three fundamental principles govern ecodevelopment:

  • Environmental changes are irreversible as a result of economic development.
  • Human economic activity takes place within a larger ecosystem, but the side effects include pollution and the depletion of natural resources;
  • the exponential growth in population is the second factor in the depletion of natural resources and an increase in the volume of waste

In order to achieve sustainable development, environmental issues must be taken into account when making socioeconomic decisions. The public sector uses environmental impact indicators when making policy decisions. For instance, when building a highway, a private stakeholder is required to plant flowers or trees along the divider. This is what is referred to as a development/environment trade-off.

Integrating environmental sustainability into development via the SDGs (Sustainable Development Goals)

In a balanced and integrated manner, the three elements of sustainable development—economic, social, and environmental—are targeted by the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development. Four policy changes are essential, according to the UN Economic and Social Commission for Asia and the Pacific (ESCAP), in order to advance integration:

  • Fundamental Policy: Social justice and environmental sustainability must move from being marginal policy aims to becoming fundamental policy goals.
  • Long-term Benefits: A move from a primarily short-term policy vision to one that seeks long-term gains for all parties is necessary.
  • GDP replacement: Metrics that take into account all three facets of sustainable development should take the place of an emphasis on Gross Domestic Product (GDP) as a gauge of advancement.
  • Limited Resources: Public policy must acknowledge that the world's resources are finite and that technology cannot always overcome resource restrictions.

Policy shifts are needed to address issues in public decision-making for the implementation of SDGs, which can reduce negative externalities such as pollution, emissions, waste and social clashes. To do this, government sector institutional frameworks and capacity-building must be strengthened, requiring political commitment and an inclusive vision. Agenda setting, policy formulation, implementation, and monitoring and evaluation are the challenges faced by governments. An integrated approach, including the use of input-output analysis and multiple indicators, exemplified by New Zealand, is needed to achieve the SDGs.
