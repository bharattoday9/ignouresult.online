Write a short note on the universal principles of sustainable development

13 Dec, 2022 | Written by Naman Kumar

I picked up the above question from IGNOU's Check Your Progress of Unit 3 of BPAG-174 book. I have answered the question below.

Sustainable development is a concept that has been gaining traction and recognition in recent years, as our world is faced with the imminent ecological crisis. The concept of sustainable development was first introduced in the 1987 report ‘Our Common Future’, commonly referred to as the Brundtland Report, which laid out the blueprint for a new way of living that would benefit both the environment and humanity. In this report, sustainable development is defined as “development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs”.

Since then, many organizations, such as the United Nations, have developed and adopted the universal principles of sustainable development to guide the decision-making and actions of governments, businesses, and citizens. These principles are centered around the three core aspects of sustainability: environmental, economic and social.

What are the Universal Principles of Sustainable Development?

I have studied the following principles of sustainable development in my course on sustainable development and have found them to be very useful in guiding my decision-making and actions:

  • Intergenerational Equity: This principle states that we must protect the interests of future generations by utilizing resources responsibly and efficiently, so that our current use of resources does not compromise the needs of future generations.
  • Participatory Decision-Making: This principle emphasizes the importance of involving all stakeholders in decision-making processes, to ensure that all voices are heard and all perspectives are considered.
  • Prevention of Unsustainable Development: This principle encourages the reduction of unsustainable activities and practices in order to ensure that our actions have a positive impact on the environment.
  • Environmental Protection: This principle calls for the protection of natural resources and ecosystems, in order to ensure the sustainability of the environment.
  • Social Equity: This principle focuses on the need to reduce inequalities within and between societies, in order to ensure the sustainability and well-being of communities.
  • Economic Efficiency: This principle encourages the efficient and effective use of resources to ensure that economic activities are sustainable over the long-term.
  • Technology Transfer: This principle promotes the sharing of information and technology between countries, in order to reduce the environmental impact of development activities.
  • Polluter Pays Principle: This principle holds those responsible for causing environmental damage to be accountable for their actions, in order to reduce the impact of unsustainable practices.
  • Sustainable Consumption and Production: This principle calls for the transition to sustainable consumption and production patterns, in order to reduce the environmental impact of our activities.
  • Ecological Integrity: This principle seeks to protect and restore ecosystems, in order to maintain the sustainability of our environment.

These universal principles of sustainable development serve as a powerful tool to guide us in our decision-making and actions. By incorporating these principles into our everyday life, we can ensure that our actions are in line with the principles of sustainability and that we are doing our part in protecting our planet and its resources.

How are the Principles of Sustainable Development Significant?

The Sustainable Development (SD) concept was developed in the 1970s, but it wasn't until the World Commission on Environment and Development (WCED, also known as the Brundtland Commission) published its Report on Our Common Future in 1987 that it became the focal point of development discourse, leading to the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) in 2015.

SDGs were established by the UN in 1992, but most countries struggled to move toward their fulfilment. In 2012, the heads of state from across the world met and created the framework for accomplishing the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs), but they were unable to establish a plan for reaching any Sustainable Development Goals.

  • It should be of a worldwide nature, addressing issues that affect all nations, not just emerging ones.
  • It should outline a widely accepted global development agenda.
  • Several important issues that the MDGs did not completely address should be included. It ought to be thorough and reflect all three SD dimensions.
  • It should take into account short-term benchmarks while having a long-term perspective and aiming for the 2030 deadline.
  • It should involve all stakeholders in resource mobilisation and implementation.
  • It should allow for the possibility of reviewing these objectives in light of developing scientific evidence (UN, 2013).

The United Nations established 17 SDGs in 2015, and 169 indicators have been created for countries to follow. 193 member countries committed to working toward the SDGs, and India is one of them.
