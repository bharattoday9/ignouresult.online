Explain the dimensions of the Community Capacity Building approach to sustainable development.

13 Dec, 2022 | Written by Naman Kumar

I have included a preffered answer to the above IGNOU question below.

Community Capacity Building (CCB) is an approach to sustainable development that focuses on strengthening the skills, abilities, and resources of individuals, organizations, and communities. It is a holistic approach to creating resilient and sustainable communities by emphasizing the development of a wide range of social, environmental, and economic resources. By nurturing the capacity of individuals, organizations, and communities to identify and address their own needs, CCB creates a platform for sustainable community development.

Dimensions of Community Capacity Building

If you don't know yet, there are multiple dimensions involve in the Community Capacity Building approach to sustainable development. I have attempted to list each of them below, and also explained them briefly. Kindly, take a note of them.

Human Resources

CCB emphasizes the development of the skills and abilities of individuals, organizations, and communities. This includes developing leadership skills, improving communication and problem-solving abilities, and creating a supportive environment for learning and sharing knowledge, futhermore providing providing access to educational opportunities, the formation of strong networks, and the promotion of collaboration between different stakeholders.

Financial Resources

The importance of economic resources in sustainable community development is also emphasied alongsiee the focus on identifying and developing appropriate economic activities, such as micro-enterprises, small business development, and local job creation. It also involves the development of financial management and budgeting skills, as well as access to financial services and investment capital.

Physical Resources

Physical resources are paid attention to in creating sustainable communities which includes the development of infrastructure and facilities, such as housing, sanitation, transportation, and energy systems alongside the protection and conservation of natural resources, such as forests, water, land, and biodiversity.

Social Resources

Development of social capital, such as trust, social networks, and shared values, including creating spaces for dialogue, mutual understanding, and respect between different stakeholders are also emphisized in Community Capacity Building.

Political Resources

The importance of political resources in creating sustainable communities becomes evident during the Capacity Building Process which includes the development of effective governance structures, such as participatory decision-making processes, legal frameworks, and access to information. It also involves the promotion of civic engagement, policy dialogue, and advocacy.

Cultural Resources

Promotion of cultural diversity, respect for local knowledge and traditions, and the protection of language and cultural heritage, AND encouraging the participation of different stakeholders in cultural activities, such as festivals and celebrations are some of the important aspects of CCB.

Detailed Info on the Community Capacity Building Approach

The Community Capacity Building (CCB) method suggests that people who are living in poverty should strengthen their skills and knowledge. So that individuals can actively participate in a community and make a positive impact on society, they must be equipped with the knowledge and skills to do so. The Ford Foundation's ABCD Model, which SD uses, intends to "Acquire, Develop, Improve, or Transfer through generations" the development of assets. SD serves the bulk of the rural population. Three key areas where the ABCD Model can be used in the CCB method are listed below:

  1. Resources: The livestock, natural resources (such as forests and animals), and financial assets can all support sustainable livelihoods. They are crucial for preserving culture and the environment by purifying, recycling, and replenishing air and water (UN, 2013).
  2. Social Bonds: Community relationships and social ties promote community involvement in helping the less fortunate.
  3. Human Assets: In the context of employment and compensation in rural areas, the term "human assets" refers to an individual's skills.

Many initiatives and programmes that promote sustainability have been successfully implemented under this model include, for instance, organising a cleaning drive by handing out a small gift to the on-the-street persons who bring empty cans and plastic bottles.

This accomplishes two goals at once, namely sustainability and helping the underprivileged. The rural poor have benefited from this type of participative CCB approach, which integrates the social, economic, and environmental facets of sustainable development.

The most vulnerable populations need to be given more influence, as stated quite clearly in the New Agenda for the Sustainable Development Goals published by the UN. All children, youth, people with disabilities (80% of whom live in poverty), elderly people, indigenous peoples, refugees, internally displaced people, and migrants are among those whose needs are reflected in the Agenda (UN, 2015).

The governments, commercial sector, UN system, and civil society will all need to work together to execute the SDGs. According to the New Agenda, the UN will collaborate with local governments and communities to renovate and design our cities and human settlements in order to promote personal security and communal cohesion, as well as to spur economic growth and creativity.

Conclusion

The Community Capacity Building approach to sustainable development is a holistic approach that emphasizes the development of a wide range of social, environmental, and economic resources. By nurturing the capacity of individuals, organizations, and communities to identify and address their own needs, CCB creates a platform for sustainable community development. It is a comprehensive approach that recognizes the importance of human, financial, physical, social, political, and cultural resources in creating resilient and sustainable communities.
