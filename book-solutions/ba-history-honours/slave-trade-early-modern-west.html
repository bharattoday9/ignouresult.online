Explain the Growth of Slave Trade during the Early Modern West in 300 words

12 Dec, 2022 | Written by Naman Kumar

Slave Trade during the Early Modern West was an integrated part of the trans-atlantic trade.

The early modern West saw a drastic increase in the trans-Atlantic slave trade. This period was marked by the rise of European colonialism and mercantilism, which created an increased demand for slave labor. This essay will discuss the various factors that contributed to the growth of the slave trade during the early modern West.

Colonialism

The establishment of European colonies in the Americas was a major factor in the growth of the slave trade. The colonists needed workers to farm and cultivate the land, as well as to work in the mines and other industries. The demand for labor drove the need to bring in slaves from Africa. In addition, the colonies served as a major market for the sale of African slaves.

Mercantilism

The rise of mercantilism also contributed to the growth of the slave trade. Mercantilism was an economic system that sought to maximize the exports of a country and minimize the imports. European countries sought to export manufactured goods and import raw materials. This created a need for more slaves to work in the plantations and mines in the colonies.

Triangular Trade

The growth of the slave trade was also facilitated by a system known as the triangular trade. This system involved the exchange of goods between Europe, Africa, and the Americas. European countries would trade manufactured goods for African slaves, who were then sold in the colonies in exchange for raw materials.

Competition among European Nations

The competition between European nations also contributed to the growth of the slave trade. European countries sought to gain a competitive advantage by controlling the slave trade and gaining access to the colonies. This led to increased demand for slaves, as each country sought to gain an economic advantage over the others.

The large-scale slave trade was another result of the Age of Exploration. For instance, over the latter half of the fifteenth century, roughly a thousand slaves were transported to Portugal annually. Domestic servants who were slaves existed before the age of exploration. However, with the discovery of the Americas in the 1490s and the construction of sugarcane plantations, the demand for slaves significantly changed. During the Middle Ages, cane sugar was brought from Southwest Asia to Europe.

Large agricultural estates known as plantations were established in the sixteenth century to cultivate sugarcane on Caribbean islands and along the Brazilian coast. The need for labour increased as a result. The few native Americans, sometimes known as AmeriIndians, had been completely wiped out by epidemic diseases brought over from Europe. As a result, slaves from Africa were sent to the Caribbean and Brazil to work on the plantations. The first boatload of African slaves to travel from Africa to the Americas was brought by a Spanish ship in 1518. The slave trade expanded during the following two centuries and joined the triangle trade that signalled the creation of a new global economy.

The triangle trade pattern linked the continents of Europe, Africa, Asia, and America. European merchant ships transported manufactured products from Europe to Africa where they were exchanged for a shipment of slaves. The slaves were later sold after being transported to the Americas. Then, European traders purchased raw cotton, tobacco, molasses, sugar, and sugar, shipping them back to Europe for resale in European marketplaces. During the sixteenth century, an estimated 275,000 African slaves were exported. Every year, 2,000 people travelled to the Americas alone.

The amount increased to over a million in the seventeenth century and over six million in the eighteenth. By that time, trade had reached East Africa from West and Central Africa. Between the sixteenth and the late nineteenth century, up to ten million African slaves were transported to the Americas. Up until a group of Christian reformers known as the Quakers started to criticise it, this institution was thought to be natural.

Conclusion

The trans-Atlantic slave trade saw a drastic increase during the early modern West. This was due to various factors, such as the establishment of European colonies in the Americas, the rise of mercantilism, the triangular trade, and the competition between European countries. All of these factors led to an increased demand for African slaves, which fueled the growth of the slave trade.
