How do you see the individual and collective identities of the early modern West?

11 Dec, 2022 | Written by Naman Kumar

The early modern West saw individuals exhibiting an individual identity based on their unique experiences, while collective identities were formed around shared values and beliefs, such as nationhood and religious faith.

The early modern West was marked by its complex and varied individual and collective identities. The period encompassed a multitude of cultures, religions, and political systems that shaped the identities of both individuals and entire societies. This essay will explore the individual and collective identities of the early modern West, examining how they developed and interacted with one another.

Individual Identities

Individual identities during the early modern period were heavily influenced by the prevailing culture, religion, and politics of the time. People often identified with a particular faith, nationality, and cultural tradition, and these identities were often closely tied to their economic and social statuses. For example, a wealthy merchant in 16th century England may have identified as an upper-class English Protestant, whereas a peasant in the same region may have identified as a lower-class Catholic.

Collective Identities

The collective identities of the early modern West were often defined by the same factors that shaped individual identities. Nations, for example, sought to establish a unified national identity that was based on shared cultural and religious traditions as well as political ideologies. This was especially true in the 16th and 17th centuries, when monarchs sought to unify and strengthen their realms by emphasizing their respective national identities. Religious groups also developed collective identities, as Protestant and Catholic churches sought to distinguish themselves from one another in terms of doctrine and practice.

The pursuit of individual and social liberty was in conflict. It is usually believed that the West is where the concept of the individual was first developed. It served as the cornerstone of Western philosophy and ethics and was fundamental to Christianity in its quest for personal salvation. Similar concepts can be found in other ancient cultures, therefore the idea of the individual as such cannot be completely assigned to the West. However, what is arguably more distinctively Western were the political ramifications that resulted from the discovery of the individual. Later, liberalism and the capitalist ethic benefited from the emphasis on the individual.

However, the underlying concept might be seen in Calvin's notion of the Calling during the Reformation. The idea of the Calling was religious in nature—it was seen as a mission assigned by God. But according to Weber, the idea of the Calling was fresh, a result of the Reformation, and a Protestant conception. The original definition of calling included the idea that doing one's duty in worldly matters was the highest shape that one's moral behaviour could take. The individual was required to fulfil the responsibilities of his or her position in the world in order to be accepted by God, giving everyday worldly obligations a religious meaning.

Luther may have been the one who first articulated the idea of calling, but according to Weber, he was not particularly sympathetic to capitalism or the capitalistic spirit. As a result, a more conventional understanding of economic activity came to predominate in Luther's teachings, which included opposition to capital and profit-making and acceptance of one's occupation and work as a divine ordinance. Contrarily, the teachings of Calvin, Wesley, and others were similarly concerned with the redemption of the soul, but these teachings had unintended repercussions. Such a mindset was not conducive to a major change in approach to economic activity.

According to Weber, the Puritan sects, reformers like Calvin, Menno, George Fox, and Wesley all saw the Calling in its most direct form in a way that was connected to the rise of the capitalistic spirit. Meaning that neither social reform nor cultural aspirations nor humanism were the foci of these writers' teachings. However, one unintended result of their teachings was to encourage the growth of the capitalistic spirit. The Huguenots of France, the Geneva Calvinists, the Dutch Reformed churches, the English and New England Puritans, and the Presbyterian Church in Scotland and North America are the most well-known branches of this tradition.

Conclusion

The early modern West was characterized by a variety of individual and collective identities. These identities were shaped by the prevailing culture, religion, and politics of the time, and they often interacted and intersected with one another in complex ways. As such, the individual and collective identities of the early modern West provide us with a unique insight into the period and a greater understanding of how identities have developed over time.
