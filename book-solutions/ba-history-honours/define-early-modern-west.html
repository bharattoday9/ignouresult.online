How do you define the term ‘Early Modern West’?

11 Dec, 2022 | Written by Naman Kumar

Early Modern West is a term used to describe the period of history between the 15th and 18th centuries. It is also known as the Early Modern Period.

Early Modern West is an era of history that marks a transitional period between the Middle Ages and the start of the modern world. It is a period of extraordinary social and economic transformation, which saw the emergence of new political institutions, the growth of international trade, and the rise of powerful nation-states.

Defining the Early Modern West

  • The Early Modern West is generally defined as the period from the 16th to the 18th centuries.
  • This period is characterized by the emergence of the nation-state, the creation of centralized governments, the expansion of global trade networks, and the growth of a commercial economy.
  • It was a period of rapid social and economic change, when scientific and technological advances transformed the landscape of Europe and the Americas.
  • This period saw the rise of European colonialism, the Protestant Reformation, and the Scientific Revolution, which all contributed to the development of the modern world.
  • It was also a period of exploration, conquest, and expansion, as Europeans sought to expand their empires around the world.
  • The Early Modern West was a period of great intellectual ferment, when scholars and scientists questioned traditional religious and cultural beliefs and sought new ways to understand the world.

Conclusion

Overall, the Early Modern West was a period of remarkable social and economic change, which laid the foundations for the modern world. It was a period of exploration and conquest, of technological and scientific advancement, and of intellectual and cultural transformation. It was a period of great political and economic upheaval, which ultimately gave rise to the modern nation-state.

Detailed Chapter Summary

In the 1970s, the phrase "early modern" became popular in history. The word was widely used, as evidenced by the well-known works of historians Natalie Zemon Davis, Society and Culture in Early Modern France (1975), Peter Burke, Popular Culture in Early Modern Europe (1972), and Economy and Society in Early Modern Europe (1978). In the context of European history, it describes a time between the mediaeval and modern periods of history and offers a solution to the periodization issue. The study of society, economy, and popular culture, which cannot be adequately explored and described within the constrictions of monarchical reigns or national events, may appeal more to people who are interested in these topics.

This period also saw a fresh emphasis placed on the use of observation as the basis of knowledge and a resurgence of interest in the writings of intellectuals from classical Greece and Rome. The Renaissance was a series of developments that eventually gave rise to a new liberal humanist philosophy that saw men—not women—as the engineers, authors of their own histories, builders of empires, and masters of language and knowledge. Early modern times saw the emergence of new ways of observing nature and conducting experiments as a means of advancing knowledge. This is demonstrated by Nicolaus Copernicus' model of the solar system, which placed the sun at the centre and the planets revolving around it.

The invention of printing using moveable type by Johannes Gutenberg (c. 1398–1468) in the 1450s helped the spread of these novel ideas. The economy saw a substantial transition as well, with fewer people holding land under the feudal system. Farmers paid rent in the form of commodities or cash, rather than receiving access to land in exchange for military duty or unpaid labour. Through criticism of its theology and practises, the Catholic Church's dominance in religion was contested, which ultimately sparked the growth of other Protestant denominations. Finally, at about the same time, Europeans learned about cultures outside of Europe; Christopher Columbus' (1451–1506) journey is the most well-known and marked the beginning of the colonisation of the Americas.

The lack of Single Power Center

One could argue that no one power ever enjoyed lasting dominance in Western history, whether during the early modern era or afterwards, when full-fledged modernity arose. This has a long-lasting, irreversible impact on how the West was created. There were numerous power centres but no unified political organisation. A variety of city states, tiny regional states, territorial empires, and later nation-states have predominated throughout Western history, but none have ever achieved complete dominance or survived for an extended period of time.

These nations differed in terms of their economies, social structures, and cultures, but they also have a lot in common. The various Western powers frequently engaged in war, but since 1648, a system of power balance has also developed. In numerous nations, there were disputes between the church and the state. However, they were also able to come to an understanding over their conflicting statements. It was a compromise where the state gained the upper hand, at least in a few early modern great absolutist states. However, just as there was no single institution or centre within society that could rule over the entire society, there was also no single centre in inter-state interactions.

The Strong Tradition of Civil Society

Due to a long legacy of civil society, which became especially prominent in the early modern West, there was no strong institution within societal order. This was a leftover from the Middle Ages. Feudalism was unable to stop municipal authorities from establishing rights against the nobility and restricting the concentration of power. A dispersed power structure and a system of reciprocal obligations were the foundations of feudalism. The ruling elites thus had to negotiate and share power with organised civil society organisations, such as merchants, craft workers, the intellectuals, and eventually organised workers, even after the establishment of absolute monarchs in a few nations.

In the independent cities in particular, this market was the most developed. There, the necessities of civic society and established guilds restrained the spread of absolutism. A foundation for legal and symbolic connections of mutual recognition where the ruler had to offer rights to those below them in exchange for their obligations was provided by the power structure that feudalism institutionalised. This was a legacy that the early modern West could not erase. This continuity is evident throughout the history of the early modern West in the form of civil society movements that had a significant impact on the course of societal development. Any political order's legality might always be contested by individuals who disagreed with it. This unquestionably had a long-term democratising effect.

Additionally, the relationship between the present and the past can be understood in terms of continuity and discontinuity from very early on in the history of the Modern West. The Middle Ages are responsible for a wide range of legacies. Periodic ruptures, however, also bring about transformation. There had long been disagreements within Christianity regarding the authority of the church and the Bible. The Church was supposed to have a primarily spiritual focus, but during the Middle Ages, it also had extensive rights over land ownership and other material advantages. Therefore, tensions were destined to arise as a result of a strong social institution being involved in a depraved society, which Max Weber argued gave the West the initial push towards rationality.

From the Middle Ages through the Renaissance and Enlightenment, movements that influenced subsequent Western history had a tendency to separate the present from the past. Undoubtedly, the Reformation and the Renaissance believed that the present's validity came from the resurgence of an older past, which, if recovered, would enable the present to distance itself from the recent past. However, this attitude of rebirth or resurgence of a more primitive mentality was a repudiation of the earlier era.

The later and more utopian movements of the seventeenth to nineteenth centuries contributed to the rise of a more forward-looking dimension in the form of the idea of progress. In this way, continuity was mostly achieved through rupture, which allowed for the rebuilding of the past in new ways.
